Swarm Drone Optimization Project Notes

Project Overview
Our optimization group project focuses on swarm drone area coverage. The goal is to make multiple drones cover a given region as effectively as possible while minimizing energy consumption. The final implementation and simulation will be done in NVIDIA Isaac Sim.

The main optimization objective is not only to maximize coverage, but also to reduce unnecessary movement, avoid overlap between drones, and keep the swarm behavior realistic enough for simulation.

Where We Started
At the beginning, we defined the general project idea as energy-efficient swarm drone coverage. The problem can be described as:

- A target region must be covered by multiple drones.
- The drones should maximize total area coverage.
- The drones should avoid covering the same area repeatedly.
- The drones should minimize total energy usage.
- The drones should avoid collisions with each other and with obstacles.
- The final behavior should be testable in NVIDIA Isaac Sim.

We also identified that the project has both an optimization part and a simulation part. The optimization part decides how the drones should move or divide the area, while the simulation part demonstrates and evaluates the behavior in Isaac Sim.

Research Phase
After defining the problem, we started researching possible algorithmic approaches. Instead of choosing one method immediately, we decided to first compare several families of methods and evaluate which ones are realistic for our project timeline and compatible with Isaac Sim.

The main families of methods we discussed were:

1. Classical coverage and geometry-based methods
   - Coverage path planning
   - Grid-based decomposition
   - Boustrophedon/lawnmower coverage
   - Voronoi-based partitioning
   - Task allocation between drones

2. Metaheuristic optimization methods
   - Particle Swarm Optimization
   - Genetic Algorithms
   - Ant Colony Optimization
   - Simulated Annealing
   - Other swarm-inspired methods such as Grey Wolf, Firefly, or Bee algorithms

3. Learning and control-based methods
   - Potential Fields
   - Consensus-Based Coordination
   - Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning
   - Model Predictive Control
   - Energy-aware reward or cost functions

Team Division
Since there are three members in the group, we divided the research by method families.

Teammate 1 will research classical coverage and geometry-based methods. Their focus is to find a simple and reliable baseline for area coverage, especially methods that are easy to implement and visualize in Isaac Sim.

Teammate 2 will research metaheuristic optimization methods. Their focus is to understand whether methods like PSO, Genetic Algorithms, Ant Colony Optimization, or Simulated Annealing can improve coverage and energy efficiency compared to a simple baseline.

I will research the third topic: learning and control-based methods. My focus is on methods that can guide swarm behavior more dynamically, especially Potential Fields, Consensus-Based Coordination, and Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning.

My Research Focus
For the learning/control-based part, we compared several possible methods using criteria such as coverage quality, energy efficiency, collision and overlap handling, scalability, Isaac Sim compatibility, implementation difficulty, and project risk.

The main methods I will focus on are:

1. Potential Fields
This is likely the simplest method among the three. The idea is that drones are attracted toward uncovered areas and repelled from obstacles, boundaries, or other drones. This method is practical because it is relatively easy to implement, easy to visualize, and suitable as a first swarm-movement baseline.

2. Consensus-Based Coordination
This method adds more swarm-level coordination. Drones use shared rules or communication to agree on spacing, direction, formation, or task distribution. It is more advanced than Potential Fields but still more manageable than full reinforcement learning.

3. Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning
This is the most advanced option. Drones learn behavior through rewards based on coverage, energy use, collision avoidance, and overlap penalties. This method is powerful, but it is also the riskiest because it requires training, reward tuning, debugging, and more computation.

Energy-Aware Objective
All three methods should be evaluated using the same energy-aware objective. For reinforcement learning, this can be called a reward function. For Potential Fields and Consensus-Based Coordination, it is better to call it a cost function or evaluation objective.

The energy-aware objective should include:

- Coverage percentage
- Total distance traveled
- Estimated energy consumption
- Overlap between drones
- Collision avoidance
- Smoothness of movement
- Time needed to complete coverage
- Scalability when increasing the number of drones

Current Plan
The plan is to test the three learning/control-based methods in increasing order of complexity:

1. Potential Fields
Start with this as the simplest baseline. It should allow us to quickly create swarm behavior where drones move toward uncovered regions while avoiding each other and obstacles.

2. Consensus-Based Coordination
After the baseline works, extend the swarm behavior by adding coordination rules. This can help drones distribute themselves better across the region and reduce repeated coverage.

3. Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning
Finally, explore MARL as the most advanced method. This should only be attempted after simpler methods are working, because it has higher implementation and training risk.

Why This Order Makes Sense
This order is realistic because it moves from simple to complex. Potential Fields can provide a working baseline quickly. Consensus-Based Coordination improves the swarm behavior without requiring full learning. Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning can then be explored as an advanced extension if time allows.

This approach also reduces project risk. Even if MARL becomes too difficult to fully complete, the project can still have a working Isaac Sim demonstration using Potential Fields or Consensus-Based Coordination.
